We see what we expect to see.
Not what's actually there.

Human perception is active, not passive. We interpret, filter, and fill in gaps, often without knowing it. Every interface you design operates inside these constraints.

Recap

Human Perception

The processes through which we interpret sensory information. It's the foundation of how users experience every interface you design.

01
Perception is Subjective
Shaped by individual experience, culture, and context. Two users can look at the same screen and interpret it differently.
02
Perception is Limited
Attention, cognitive biases, and environmental distractions all constrain what users actually notice in your interface.

What limits what users see

Limitation 01
Change
Blindness

Users fail to notice changes in their visual environment, even significant ones, especially when the change occurs during a scroll, transition, blink, or distraction.

Change Blindness: Design Implications

Designing against it

  • 01 Use visual cues to signal changes. Color, size, motion, and sound direct attention toward updates or state changes users would otherwise miss.
  • 02 Slow or peripheral changes go unnoticed. Users rarely catch changes that occur gradually or outside their immediate focus, even significant ones.
  • 03 Task switches amplify the effect. Visual disruptions and context switches increase change blindness. Minimize interruptions. Make transitions continuous, not abrupt.

Limitation 03

Selective Attention

Focusing on specific stimuli while filtering out everything else. Users can only consciously process a small amount of information at once.

  • → Focus is limited. Prioritize key elements. Minimize visual noise and competing calls to action.
  • → Overload impairs performance. Too much competing information overwhelms users. Simplify layouts and use visual hierarchy to guide attention to what matters.

Limitation 04
Banner
Blindness

Users consciously or unconsciously ignore information that visually resembles advertising, even when that information is genuinely useful or important.

Banner Blindness: Design Implications

Three things to know

  • 01 Don't put critical content in ad zones. Avoid placing important information or CTAs where users expect ads. unless it actually is an ad.
  • 02 Ad-like visuals trigger the filter. Flashy colors, animation, and typical ad copy get tuned out. even if the content has nothing to do with advertising.
  • 03 Relevance cuts through. Personalized or contextually appropriate content captures attention regardless of format or placement.

Banner Blindness

Proximity to ads is also an issue

Content placed near known ad zones gets caught in the same filter. Even if it isn't an ad, its location makes users treat it like one.

Design implication: Important content (warnings, recommendations, key actions) should be placed away from traditional ad real estate. Right rail, top banner, and between-content slots all carry heavy filter bias.

Scanning Patterns

Users scan. They almost never read.

Eye-tracking research shows that users move their eyes in predictable patterns based on content type, layout, and goal. Knowing these patterns helps you place the right information in the right place.

NN/g's 2017 follow-up research documented over 25 distinct scanning patterns. The four covered here are the most commonly designed for, but the full picture is more varied. Users with strong task focus often deviate from any single pattern.

Pattern 01

F-Pattern

Users read horizontally across the top, then scan down the left side, making progressively shorter horizontal sweeps.

  • → Most reliable for text-heavy content: search results, article feeds, listings.
  • → Less universal than originally framed. Users with strong task focus or high content interest frequently deviate from it.
Pattern 02

Z-Pattern

The eye sweeps horizontally across the top, then diagonally down to the bottom-left, then horizontally across again, tracing a Z shape.

  • → Common on pages with limited body text and strong visual hierarchy: landing pages, hero sections, posters.
  • → Useful when you want the eye to naturally arrive at a call to action at the bottom right.
Pattern 03

Spotted Pattern

Users look for specific items or keywords, skipping irrelevant content entirely. Their eye jumps between visual anchors: headings, images, labels, prices.

  • → Design for this with strong visual hierarchy and clear labeling. Scale, color, animation, and imagery all serve as anchors.
  • → Common in ecommerce, searchable databases, and reference sites.
Pattern 04

Commitment Pattern

Users read most or all of the content, every word in every line. This only happens when users are genuinely motivated to absorb the full piece.

  • → Common on news sites, long-form editorial, magazine features, and technical documentation.
  • → Design for it with legible typefaces, strong contrast, and comfortable line length (~60–70 characters).
Scanning Patterns

Patterns shift on mobile

The F-pattern assumes a wide viewport with long horizontal text lines. On mobile, that rarely applies. The screen is narrow, the scroll is vertical, and the thumb governs what gets attention first.

01
Thumb Zone Governs Attention
What users can comfortably reach shapes where attention naturally falls. Center and lower-center of the screen tend to win.
02
Spotted Pattern Dominates
Swipe-based interfaces (feeds, carousels, TikTok-style layouts) train users to scan for visual anchors rather than read linearly.
03
Z-Pattern Is Difficult
The narrow viewport makes Z-pattern layouts hard to design effectively. Most mobile layouts push toward single-column sequential reading instead.
Looking Ahead

AI is changing how users scan New

Traditional scanning pattern research was conducted on static layouts. AI-integrated interfaces are introducing perceptual dynamics those studies didn't anticipate.

  • 01 AI summaries change what users skip. When AI generates a summary at the top, users read it and skip the body. even when the body contains important nuance.
  • 02 Personalized feeds break layout assumptions. Ranked and filtered content changes what users expect to see where, invalidating assumptions based on static layout research.
  • 03 Test, don't assume. As you design AI-integrated products, verify whether users are actually reading what you expect, or skipping past it entirely.
