Human-System Dialog & Key UX Laws

Introduction

Human-system dialog refers to the full range of interactions between a person and a digital system. Every tap, swipe, voice command, and visual response is part of an ongoing conversation.

This week: how dialog is structured, what makes it effective, and the UX laws that shape how users perceive and respond to system behavior.

Methods of Dialogue

Input Methods
How users communicate with systems: keyboard, touch, voice, gesture, sensor input. Each mode carries different affordances and constraints for the designer.
Output Methods
How systems respond: screen visuals, audio feedback, haptic response, ambient displays. Output closes the feedback loop and completes the dialog.
Design Goal
Minimize friction between intent and action. The best dialog feels like natural conversation, not a command-line exchange.

4 Design Principles

Clarity
Plain language, no jargon, clear visual hierarchy. Every label and instruction should be immediately understandable without effort.
User Agency
Give users control. Let them navigate freely, undo actions, adjust settings. Users who feel in control are more confident and less frustrated.
Consistency
Predictable patterns throughout the system. When the interface behaves consistently, users build accurate mental models faster.
Accessibility
Design for diverse abilities. Consider contrast, font size, touch targets, and screen readers. Good accessibility helps everyone.

Dialog Design Principles

Feedback
Every action should have a visible response. Users need to know the system heard them: immediately, clearly, and without ambiguity.
Decision Making
Reduce cognitive load by presenting information progressively. Support decisions rather than overwhelming users with all options at once.
Learnability
Design so first-time users can succeed without documentation. Patterns and conventions should feel familiar even to new users.

Hick's Law

Interactive
The Law
Decision time grows logarithmically with the number of choices. More options → slower decisions, but not linearly.
Formula
RT = b × log₂(n + 1). As choices double, time grows predictably. Use this to design smarter navigation.
Design Rule
Limit visible options. Chunk navigation. Progressive disclosure is Hick's Law applied.

Paradox of Choice

The Problem
More choices don't always lead to better outcomes. An abundance of options causes decision paralysis and post-decision regret, even when users get what they wanted.
The Research
Iyengar & Lepper's jam study: 24 choices drew attention but 6 choices drove 10× more purchases. Fewer options, better conversion.
The Fix
Curate, don't dump. Help users narrow choices progressively. Use defaults, recommendations, and filtering to reduce the decision burden.

Progressive Disclosure

The Principle
Show only what the user needs for the current task. Reveal complexity progressively, on demand. Don't front-load everything into the first screen.
Why It Works
Reduces cognitive load at the point of entry. Keeps the primary path clean. Advanced users can dig deeper; novices aren't overwhelmed.
Where to Apply
Settings screens, form design, navigation menus, feature discovery. Any interface where a subset of functionality covers most use cases.

Fitts' Law

Distance & Size
Time to acquire a target is a function of distance and size. Far away or small = significantly longer to select accurately.
Error Risk
Small targets increase error rates. When users miss, they must recover, adding frustration and time to every interaction.
Target Size
Make interactive elements large enough to tap easily. On mobile, targets should be at least 44×44pt. Larger = faster and more accurate.
Positioning
Place frequent controls close to where the user's pointer or thumb naturally rests. Distance is a tax: minimize it.

Accessible Areas

Thumb zone analysis

Natural thumb reach creates comfort zones on mobile screens. Bottom-center is easiest. Top corners are hardest.

Design primary actions into the thumb-friendly zones. Put destructive actions at the top.

Dark Patterns

What They Are
UI/UX choices deliberately crafted to manipulate users into actions they didn't intend: signing up, overpaying, sharing data. The design is working against the user.
How They Work
They exploit the same principles we've been studying. Fitts' Law weaponized: make the "Cancel" target tiny and far away. Hick's Law weaponized: bury the opt-out in 12 confusing options.
Why It Matters
Recognizing dark patterns is a professional obligation. As designers, you will be asked to implement them. Knowing what they are, and why they erode trust, is how you push back.

The Doherty Threshold

The Law
System response time must be under 400ms to keep users engaged and in flow. Slower responses cause users to disengage from the task and shift attention.
Why It Matters
When response times exceed 400ms, users perceive the system as slow. Engagement drops, errors increase, and users lose their place in the task flow.
Animations
Use animations as feedback, not just decoration. A well-timed progress indicator or skeleton screen keeps users oriented during longer operations.

The 400ms Rule

The Number
400 milliseconds is the threshold for perceived immediacy. At or below 400ms, responses feel instantaneous. Above it, users feel the wait consciously.
Real World
Network latency, rendering time, and JS execution all contribute to total response time. Measure end-to-end, not just server response.
What To Do
Optimize critical rendering paths. Use optimistic UI updates. Show loading states immediately. Never leave users with zero feedback, even briefly.
